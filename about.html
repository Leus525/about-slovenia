  • Kurenti

    Kurentovanje is one of Slovenia's most popular and ethnologically significant carnival events. This 10-day rite of spring and fertility is celebrated on Shrove Sunday in Ptuj, the oldest documented city in the region, and draws around 10,000 participants each year.

The History of Slovenia

Slovenia's independence

On 25 June 1991, the Slovenian Parliament adopted a constitutional law for the implementation of the Basic Constitutional Charter on the Sovereignty and Independence of Slovenia, the Declaration of Independence and several laws with which Slovenia assumed previous competencies of the federation on its territory. The day after independence, the Yugoslav People's Army attempted to suppress Slovene independence by force. This led to a ten-day war, in which the Slovenian TO and the police defeated the YPA. The YPA leadership had to accept the defeat and, despite serious threats and ultimatums, had to abandon the campaign in Slovenia and sit down for the negotiating table. Negotiations on the Brioni, which ended with the signing of the Brioni Declaration, began. On July 18, the SFRY Presidency adopted a decision to remove the YPA with weapons and equipment from Slovenia, and in October 1991 left the last Yugoslav soldier from Slovenia.

In November, the Denationalization Act was passed, and a new constitution was adopted in December. The European Union recognized the Republic of Slovenia in mid-January 1992 and accepted the UN among members in May 1992. Demos broke up, in April 1992, a vote of no confidence in Lojze Petrlet was passed, followed by Janez Drnovšek, who remained in power even after the 1992 and 1997 elections The mandate of the President of the Republic was given to Milan Kučan (1992-1997 and 1997-2002) twice a year. In February 1999, an association agreement with the European Union entered into force, Slovenia became a member of NATO on 29 March 2004, and on 1 May 2004 it became a member of the European Union. On 1 January 2007, Slovenia took over the euro's common currency. In the first half of 2008, she chaired the European Council.

The Celts and the Romans

In the 4th and 3rd centuries BC n. no. the territory of today's Slovenia occupied the tribes of the Celts, who established the first state formation called the Norican Kingdom. In the Gorenjska and Styria regions, Tavriski lived, Karni settled in the northwest, Noričani in the north, Japoda in Dolenjska and partly in Notranjska, Latobika in Dolenjska and Posavje, and Histri in Istria. These people lagged behind the Romans for development and economic growth. They were mainly engaged in agriculture, livestock production and pastry. From that time, the names of many of today's places (Bohinj, Tuhinj) and rivers (Sava, Savinja, Drava) originate. The cities of Emona, Celeia, Poetovio are also listed on Peutinger's Tabula showing the road network of the Roman Empire in the 1st century BC. Today's Slovene territory has had a great strategic significance for the Romans. Roman penetration into the territory of today's Slovenia began after the foundation of Aquileia (181 BC). In the second half of the 2nd century, the Romans conquered the Histri, Tavriske (129 BC) and Karne (115 BC) in the Istrian wars. Then Octavian was in the years 35-33 BC. n. no. the border was moved to the Danube and around 16 BC. n. no. peacefully joins the Allied kingdom of Norway. Occupied areas were incorporated by the Romans into a provincial organization: western Slovenia to Atrans with Istria was included in the Region 10 of Italy (Regio X), the eastern part and Dolenjska were included in the province of Pannonia, the Alpine region and Posavje were included in the middle of the 1st century in the province of Norwich. The towns of Emona (now Ljubljana), Celeia (today Celje), Poetovio (today Ptuj) and Neviodunum (now Drnovo) started to emerge. They were settled by immigrants from Italy and Romanesque Indigenous people. A road system with the main axis of Aquileia-Emona-Celeia-Poetovio-Carnuntum (Petronell by the Danube) was built.

The period between the two wars

Main article: History of Slovenia from 1918 to 1945. After the defeat of the Austro-Hungarian Empire and the Central Powers, the Croatian Parliament in Zagreb and the People's Day in Ljubljana on October 29, 1918 proclaimed the national liberation and the establishment of an independent state of Slovenes, Croats and Serbs with its center in Zagreb.

The dangers of Italy, which occupied Primorsko and Istro and parts of Dalmatia on the basis of the London Agreement of 1915, undefined borders, the absence of international recognition and the pressure [dubious - see the discussion page] of Serbs after the merger into the common state, resulted on December 1, 1918 the merger of the State of SHS with the Kingdom of Serbia into the Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes, which, since 3 October 1929, was called the Kingdom of Yugoslavia. In 1919, Yugoslav troops occupied Prekmurje and joined the kingdom. After the lost Carinthian plebiscite in 1920, southern Carinthia became Austria. After the signing of the Treaty of Rapallo (1920), a border with Italy was established, which received the whole of western Slovenia. On 1 November 1918, Major Rudolf Maister proclaimed Maribor and the Lower Styria for the possession of the State of SHS and took over the military command over Maribor and all of Lower Styria to ensure the belonging of Lower Styria to Slovenia in the barracks of the 26th Regiment's Regiment in Melju, in front of the City Commander, Lieutenant Colonel Anton Holik.
